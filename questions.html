Questions & Answers

Business Intelligence
What is Business Intelligence (BI)? ▼

Business Intelligence (BI) refers to technologies, processes, and practices used to collect, integrate, analyze, and present business data to support better decision-making.

Why is Business Intelligence important? ▼

BI helps organizations make data-driven decisions by providing insights into business performance, identifying trends, and uncovering opportunities for growth and optimization. It helps to stay ahead of competitors.

What are goals of BI? ▼

Key goals of BI include improving decision-making, increasing operational efficiency, identifying market trends, enhancing customer satisfaction, and boosting profitability.

What are the main components of a BI system? ▼

The main components include:

  • Raw data collection
  • ELT processes to transform data
  • Data warehousing / data marts
  • Data analysis and exploration
  • Reporting and visualization
  • Decision-making processes
  • AI

What types of analytics are included in BI? ▼

The types of analytics in BI are:

  • Descriptive Analytics: Understanding what happened.
  • Diagnostic Analytics: Analyzing why it happened.
  • Predictive Analytics: Predicting future trends.
  • Prescriptive Analytics: Suggesting actions based on predictions.
  • (Cognitive Analytics: Leveraging AI to derive insights.)

What is Operational Business Intelligence (OBI)? ▼

OBI focuses on real-time or near-real-time data and insights to support operational decision-making, often in dynamic or time-sensitive environments.

What are common pitfalls in implementing BI? ▼

Common pitfalls include:

  • Lack of data-driven culture
  • Data silos between departments
  • Organizational silos limiting collaboration
  • Mismatch between data insights and business goals
  • Shadow BI practices leading to inconsistent data quality
  • Technology lag and outdated systems

What is Shadow BI? ▼

Shadow BI refers to unsanctioned BI practices where individuals or departments use their own tools and processes without involving the central BI team, often leading to inconsistent and unverified data usage.

How does BI support decision-making? ▼

BI supports decision-making by providing actionable insights, historical data analysis, trend identification, and predictive modeling to guide strategic and operational actions.

What is the relationship between BI and data-driven culture? ▼

BI is most effective in organizations with a data-driven culture, where data is prioritized and actively used in decision-making processes across all levels.

What are some real-world examples of BI usage? ▼

Examples include:

  • Netflix analyzing viewer preferences to recommend content and plan future productions.
  • Amazon optimizing its supply chain using demand forecasting.
  • Airlines using BI to set dynamic pricing based on booking trends.

How does BI help with customer segmentation? ▼

BI analyzes customer data such as demographics, purchasing patterns, and behavior to group customers into segments, allowing targeted marketing and personalized experiences.

What are the benefits of BI for operational efficiency? ▼

BI improves operational efficiency by:

  • Automating reporting processes.
  • Identifying bottlenecks in workflows.
  • Optimizing resource allocation based on historical data.

What is the role of data visualization in BI? ▼

Data visualization makes complex data easier to understand by presenting it in graphs, charts, and dashboards. This helps decision-makers quickly identify trends, outliers, and insights.

What challenges do organizations face when implementing BI? ▼

Challenges include:

  • Data quality and integration issues.
  • High implementation costs.
  • Lack of skilled personnel to manage and analyze data.
  • Resistance to adopting data-driven decision-making.

How can BI support competitive advantage? ▼

BI helps businesses gain a competitive advantage by:

  • Providing insights into market trends.
  • Optimizing pricing strategies.
  • Enhancing customer experiences through tailored solutions.

What are key considerations for data governance in BI? ▼

Key considerations include:

  • Defining data ownership and responsibilities.
  • Ensuring data quality and consistency.
  • Implementing security measures to protect sensitive data.
  • Establishing clear policies for data access and usage.

What is the difference between descriptive and predictive analytics? ▼

Descriptive analytics focuses on analyzing historical data to understand past performance, while predictive analytics uses statistical models and algorithms to forecast future trends and outcomes.

Why are data silos problematic for BI? ▼

Data silos prevent seamless data integration, leading to incomplete analyses, limited insights, and inefficiencies in decision-making processes.

What is the decision-action gap in BI? ▼

The decision-action gap occurs when organizations fail to act on data-driven insights due to hesitation, lack of trust in the data, or resistance to change.

What is the relationship between BI and data warehouses? ▼

Data warehouses serve as a central repository for structured data that BI tools use for analysis, reporting, and visualization. They ensure data consistency and accessibility for BI processes.

How does Netflix use BI to optimize content? ▼

Netflix uses BI to analyze viewing patterns, engagement metrics, and content performance, enabling them to create, license, and recommend content that maximizes viewer retention and satisfaction.

How can BI assist in resource allocation? ▼

BI assists in resource allocation by analyzing historical usage patterns, forecasting future needs, and identifying areas where resources can be optimized to improve efficiency and reduce waste.

What is the role of feedback loops in BI? ▼

Feedback loops in BI involve using data insights to implement changes, then analyzing the impact of those changes to continuously refine strategies and processes.

What is the OODA loop, and how does it relate to BI? ▼

The OODA loop (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) is a decision-making model that aligns with BI processes by using data (Observe) to understand situations (Orient), make informed decisions (Decide), and take action (Act).

Database Architecture
Why isn’t a file system sufficient for storing information? ▼

File systems lack optimization for certain tasks, trade-offs in read and write speeds, and features like ACID guarantees or indexing for complex data operations.

What is the “no free lunch” theorem in database systems? ▼

Optimizing databases for certain tasks, like fast read operations, often results in trade-offs, such as slower write operations, and vice versa.

What is a database? ▼

A database is a structured system for efficient CRUD operations, ensuring ACID compliance (relational databases), storage efficiency, and scalability.

What are CRUD operations? ▼

CRUD stands for Create, Read, Update, and Delete, which are the basic operations performed on data in a database.

What is a transaction? ▼

A transaction is unit of (multiple) operations.

What is Atomicity? ▼

Atomicity ensures that a transaction is treated as an indivisible unit of work. It either fully completes or does not execute at all.

What is Consistency in databases? ▼

Consistency ensures that a database moves from one valid state to another, maintaining integrity constraints / rules.

What does Isolation guarantee? ▼

Isolation ensures that transactions execute independently of each other, avoiding conflicts or interference.

What is Durability? ▼

Durability ensures that once a transaction is committed, its changes are permanent, even in the event of a system failure.

What is indexing in a database? ▼

Indexing involves creating auxiliary data structures that improve query performance, such as faster lookups for specific column values.

What is a primary key? ▼

A primary key is an index on a column, which uniquely identifies each row in a table and ensures there are no duplicate entries.

What is a foreign key? ▼

A foreign key establishes a relationship between two tables by referencing the primary key of another table.

What are the advantages of document databases? ▼

They offer flexible schema, hierarchical data storage, scalability, and performance optimization for key-value structures.

Why use relational databases? ▼

Relational databases are ideal for strong transactional requirements, complex queries, and stable data models.

What is the purpose of blob/object storage databases? ▼

Blob storage is optimized for storing unstructured data as objects with metadata, ideal for media files, backups, and archives.

What is the difference between structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data? ▼

Structured data fits into predefined schemas (e.g., tables), semi-structured data has loose structures (e.g., JSON), and unstructured data lacks inherent organization (e.g., videos, images).

What is SQL? ▼

SQL (Structured Query Language) is a standard language for managing and querying relational databases.

What is the difference between a database and a file system? ▼

A file system organizes and stores files, focusing on metadata and simple file operations, while a database enables efficient CRUD operations, indexing, and guarantees like ACID compliance.

Why is accessing data by row index fast? ▼

Accessing data by row index is fast because it directly maps to memory locations, making it an O(1) operation.

What is binary search, and why is it efficient? ▼

Binary search is an algorithm that efficiently finds a target value in a sorted list by repeatedly dividing the search interval in half, with a time complexity of O(log(n)).

What is a one-to-many relationship in relational databases? ▼

A one-to-many relationship occurs when a single record in Table A is related to multiple records in Table B, such as a customer having multiple orders.

What is a many-to-many relationship in relational databases? ▼

A many-to-many relationship occurs when multiple records in Table A are related to multiple records in Table B, typically using a linking table.

What are cascading actions in foreign keys? ▼

Cascading actions automatically propagate changes in the parent table to the child table, such as ON DELETE CASCADE to delete related child records when a parent record is deleted.

Why are primary keys important in relational databases? ▼

Primary keys ensure data uniqueness, enable efficient lookups, and maintain data integrity by providing a unique identifier for each record.

What is the purpose of normalization in relational databases? ▼

Normalization organizes data into tables to reduce redundancy and improve data integrity by dividing data into related tables.

What is denormalization, and when is it used? ▼

Denormalization involves combining tables to reduce the complexity of queries and improve read performance, often used in data warehouses or read-intensive systems.

What are document databases optimized for? ▼

Document databases are optimized for hierarchical, nested, and flexible schema data structures, making them ideal for applications with evolving requirements.

What are the advantages of schema-less databases? ▼

Schema-less databases allow for flexible and rapid changes to the data model without migrations, suitable for dynamic and rapidly evolving applications.

What are the core differences between relational and document databases? ▼

Relational databases use structured tables and schemas with ACID compliance, while document databases use flexible, nested document structures optimized for semi-structured data.

What is a database schema? ▼

A schema defines the structure of a database, including tables, columns, data types, and constraints, ensuring data consistency and integrity.

Why is scalability important in database systems? ▼

Scalability ensures that a database can handle increased data volumes or traffic by scaling vertically (adding resources) or horizontally (adding nodes).

What are the ACID criteria? ▼

ACID stands for Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, and Durability, which ensure reliable database transactions.

Data management architecture
What is a Data Warehouse? ▼

A Data Warehouse is a central hub for storing structured data, optimized for fast querying and analysis, using processes like ETL to organize data into easily accessible formats.

What are the advantages of a Data Warehouse? ▼

Advantages include transparent ETL processes, high query performance, robust data security, and support for Business Intelligence analysis.

What are the disadvantages of a Data Warehouse? ▼

Disadvantages include high costs for schema changes, lack of support for exploratory tasks on raw data, and difficulty in troubleshooting.

What is a Data Lake? ▼

A Data Lake is a central repository for storing all kinds of data in its raw format, enabling flexibility for data exploration, machine learning, and long-term data storage.

What are the advantages of a Data Lake? ▼

Advantages include high flexibility for data formats and types, scalability for large data volumes, and cost efficiency for storage.

What are the disadvantages of a Data Lake? ▼

Disadvantages include higher costs for processing data, lack of structure leading to potential "data swamps," and security challenges due to vast and varied data.

What is a Hybrid Data Lake? ▼

A Hybrid Data Lake combines the strengths of Data Warehouses and Data Lakes, balancing structured querying with flexibility, but at the cost of increased complexity.

When is it advisable to use a Hybrid Data Lake? ▼

Hybrid Data Lakes are suitable for scenarios requiring high performance with structured data alongside the flexibility of raw data storage, such as operational BI combined with data science workflows.

What is a Lakehouse? ▼

A Lakehouse is a data architecture combining features of Data Warehouses and Data Lakes, offering a simpler design than Hybrid Data Lakes while still supporting structured querying and flexible storage.

What are the disadvantages of a Lakehouse? ▼

Disadvantages include dependency on standalone query engines and potential trade-offs in latency or costs compared to highly optimized architectures.

What is on-premise? ▼

On-premise means that managing you manage all levels, e.g. networking, data or application, yourself.

What are the different levels of Cloud services? ▼

The levels include IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service), PaaS (Platform as a Service), and SaaS (Software as a Service), each providing increasing levels of abstraction and convenience.

What are high-cost drivers in a pure Data Warehouse architecture? ▼

High-cost drivers include storage and processing of large data volumes, schema changes requiring reloading external data sources, and maintenance of structured data marts.

What are potential risks of a poorly managed Data Lake? ▼

Risks include turning into a "data swamp" with disorganized and unmanageable data, lack of transparency, and security vulnerabilities.

What are key differences between a Hybrid Data Lake and a Lakehouse? ▼

Hybrid Data Lakes offer low-latency querying with structured data and flexibility but are complex to manage, while Lakehouses reduce complexity while providing sufficient performance for most use cases.

What trade-offs exist when designing a data management architecture? ▼

Trade-offs include balancing performance with cost, flexibility with complexity, and scalability with maintainability, depending on the specific use case requirements.

Why are modular designs important in data architectures? ▼

Modular designs allow for easier maintenance, scalability, and adaptability to changes, enabling components to be updated or replaced without disrupting the entire system.

Neural Networks
What are the crucial steps in training a model? ▼

Upon training a model one needs to specify

  1. The model itself with model parameters ("weights")
  2. A loss function
Training amounts to finding those model parameters that minimize the loss function.

What is a model in general? ▼

A model is a prescription that takes input features \(x_1, \dots, x_n\) and assigns a prediction for the target \(\hat y\).

What is a loss function? ▼

A loss function compares the true target value \( y\) with the predicted target value from the model \(\hat y\).

Which data are needed for training? ▼

For training samples of the features and the true target values are needed. The latter is necessary for computing the loss function.

Which data are needed for prediction? ▼

For making a prediction with a trained model only the features of a new sample need to be knwon.

What is the univeral approximation theorem for deep neural networks ▼

A deep feedforward neural network with

  • At least one hidden layer,
  • sufficiently large width and depth,
  • non-linear activation functions
can approximate (almost) any function

What is a deep neural network? ▼

A deep neural network can be thought of a generalization of a linear regression by adding additional hidden layers.

How does a deep neural network compare to a linar regression ▼
  • Hidden layers: neural networks encorporate hidden layers. This allows to fit the data by a more flexible curve (instead of a line in the case of linear regression)
  • Loss funcitons: neural networks allow to use loss functions tailored to the problem at hand. In contrast the linear regression 'hard wires' the loss (mean squared error loss).
What is a network architecture? ▼

A network architecture is the model specification. It consists of

  • Input layer
  • Hidden layers
  • Output layer
The depth of a neural network is the total number of layers (including input and output layer). The width of a layer is the number of nodes (neurons) of that layer.

What is the gradient descent optimizer? ▼

The gradient descent optimizer finds the minimum of a function by iteratively going along the negative direction of the first derivative (gradient) of that function.

How are optimizers used for neural networks? ▼

For neural networks optimizers are used to find those values for the weights and biases \(\{W, b\}\), for which the loss function is minimized.

Is an optimizer strictly necessary for linear regression? ▼

No! While one could use an optimzer to find the optimal weights for a linear regression it is not stictly necessary. The reason is that for a linear regression the optimal weights can be computed analytically (pen and paper).

Is an optimizer necessary for neural networks in general? ▼

Yes! Upon adding hidden layers the network's weights and biases \(\{W, b\}\) that minimize the loss function cannot be found analytically (pen and paper). Therefore we need an optimzer.

What is back-propagation in the context of neural networks ▼

Back-propagation is an algorithm to determine the first derivates (gradients) of the network's weights and biases \(\{W, b\}\) with respect to the loss functions. The first derivatives are then used within an optimizer (gradient descent) to find those values of \(\{W, b\}\) that minimize the loss function.

What is an activation function? ▼

An activation function is a non-linear function that is applied on every hidden layer in the neural network.

Why do we need activation functions for neural networks? ▼

Without activation functions adding hidden layers would have no effect. They would 'collapse'.

Name a view commonly used activation functions ▼
  • Sigmoid function
  • Relu function
  • Swish function
  • Gelu function
What is overfitting? ▼

Overfitting means that the model learns the training data too well. It then captures noise and details of the training data that don't generalize to unseen data.

How can overfitting be detected? ▼

By splitting the data into training and test data. The model is only trained over trainng data. Overfitting occurs if the loss on the training data is low but the loss on the test data is high.

What can be done against overfitting for neural networks? ▼

  • More data
  • Early stopping
  • Regularization

What are the advantages of deep neural networks? ▼

Deep neural network can

  • Can systematically benefit from more data
  • Are flexible (universal approximation theorem)
  • Allow to use a loss function that is tailored to the problem at hand.

What are the disadvantes of deep neural networks? ▼

  • May become computationally expensive for large models
  • May be tedious to optimize
  • Takes some time to specify the architecture
